Interview With Dr.Thomas Arnold

Interviewed by By Abigail Hingle, October 2014

Dr. Thomas Arnold is well-known around the Indiana Academy for his thoughtful Foundations of the American Experience lectures and his in-depth knowledge of Ancient Greek philosophers, but most students don’t have much insight on his colorful background. Believe it or not, Dr. Arnold always had a strong connection with history. When I asked him if he had ever thought about doing anything other than being a history teacher, he responded by saying, “In retrospect, I was pretty much doomed to be a historian. From boyhood on, I was always interested in history. I won the fourth grade Daughters of the American Revolution history prize, and in college, from early on, I enjoyed my history classes...” Although, at some points in his educational career, he did have a few other occupations in mind.

“Growing up, I thought about a lot of different directions. In third grade I wanted to be a marine biologist... At one point, I was very interested in archaeology and the whole Indiana Jones dream. In college, for a while, I thought about being an architecturist...”
However, all of these dreams somehow fell short. In response to my question about having any regrets, Dr. Arnold said that he eventually decided that the idea of archaeology being a glamorous career is incorrect, and he absolutely didn’t regret not becoming a marine biologist, because of the “fish smell.” Next, I questioned him on why he decided to start hosting Faculty Film Nights at the Indiana Academy. “One thing I’ve noticed when talking to Academy students is that they haven’t seemed to have seen a lot of movies, a lot of films... It can be entertaining, satisfying; it can be light and lively, but it can also make you think. So I would like to help encourage that,” remarks Dr. Arnold. Another reason, Dr. Arnold started film nights was to teach Indiana Academy students that they should remain “critically engaged” even during their downtime. I went on to ask him about his professional career. Dr. Arnold has a bachelor’s degree from Oberlin College and a PhD from Ohio State. He has taught history at Yale and was a program manager for Teaching at the Alliance for Lifelong Learning in New York City. It was only natural to wonder how on earth he wound up teaching at a small high school in Indiana. “Muncie, Indiana is my wife’s hometown. I’ve been periodically visiting Muncie since before I was married in 1990...” Dr. Arnold goes on to explain that when his prior job at the Alliance for Lifelong Learning ended, he had a lot of decisions to make and plenty of time to make them. He explored different ideas such as traveling around different deserts with his wife and kids, but fate had something else in mind for him. On a trip to visit his wife’s parents, he came across one of his in-laws’ neighbors, who at the time was the director of the Indiana Academy. After a short chat, he was persuaded to do an interview for the job. With much hesitation, Dr. Arnold applied for the position. When he was hired, he imagined it as a short-term job, but ended up enjoying teaching young, gifted students so much that he stayed for over a decade. While we were speaking of his career at the Academy, I asked him what he thought of the proposed history curriculum. He answered that he likes the Foundations of the American Experience curriculum, because he gets to link American history to philosophers and events from around the world. Dr. Arnold stated that isolating American history from other world events doesn’t work in today’s modern schools. Carrying on with the vocational questions, I asked him about his book, The Renaissance at War. The title of the book is self- explanatory; the book is about warfare during the Renaissance era. In the introduction, Dr. Arnold wrote that he was influenced by colleagues, professors, and The Art of War, a book that attempts to reconcile the differences between modern and ancient military practices. I went on to ask him what he specifically found so interesting about warfare. Dr. Arnold answered, “I grew up in a military family. My father was a naval officer... My grandfather, my mother’s father, was a career officer who fought in the First World War and the Second World War. So, I grew up around people and in a family that had and were directly participating in war...” Dr. Arnold also said he finds war fascinating because it’s terrible, yet unavoidable. He even went so far as to call it a “cultural and social disease.” My last question for Dr. Arnold was, “If you could go back in time and tell your eighteen year old self one thing, what would it be?” He answered, “Have more courage in yourself...”

The Final Frontier

There can be no thought of finishing for ‘aiming for the stars.’ Both figuratively and literally, it is a task to occupy the generations. And no matter how much progress one makes, there is always the thrill of just beginning.

There can be no thought of finishing for ‘aiming for the stars.’ Both figuratively and literally, it is a task to occupy the generations. And no matter how much progress one makes, there is always the thrill of just beginning.

The dreams of yesterday are the hopes of today and the reality of tomorrow. Science has not yet mastered prophecy. We predict too much for the next year and yet far too little for the next ten.

Spaceflights cannot be stopped. This is not the work of any one man or even a group of men. It is a historical process which mankind is carrying out in accordance with the natural laws of human development.

Reaching for the Stars

As we got further and further away, it [the Earth] diminished in size. Finally it shrank to the size of a marble, the most beautiful you can imagine. That beautiful, warm, living object looked so fragile, so delicate, that if you touched it with a finger it would crumble and fall apart. Seeing this has to change a man.

To go places and do things that have never been done before – that’s what living is all about.

Space, the final frontier. These are the voyages of the Starship Enterprise. Its five-year mission: to explore strange new worlds, to seek out new life and new civilizations, to boldly go where no man has gone before.

As I stand out here in the wonders of the unknown at Hadley, I sort of realize there’s a fundamental truth to our nature, Man must explore, and this is exploration at its greatest.
